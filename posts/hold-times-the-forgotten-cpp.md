Between major unit operations, materials often sit: in tanks, bags, vessels, filters, or containers. Those waits — process **hold times** — can quietly erode product quality through chemical degradation, aggregation, or microbial growth if they are not understood and controlled.

Regulators define process hold times as the established periods during which intermediates, bulk products, media, or buffers may be held under specified conditions while remaining within defined specifications. EMA, WHO, and other agencies expect critical step durations and hold times to be stated, justified, and supported by data, not by habit. Yet in many CMC strategies, hold times are treated as scheduling details rather than as parameters that can directly impact CQAs.

Industry experience shows that poorly defined or poorly managed hold times contribute to batch failures, rework, and deviations — including microbial excursions, shifts in impurity profiles, and loss of activity. Some analyses estimate that a meaningful fraction of rejected or reworked batches can be traced back to storage and hold‑time issues, from inadequate temperature control to unclear maximum durations.

A more robust approach recognises hold times as **critical process parameters** in their own right:

* Evaluating chemical and microbiological stability of intermediates and solutions under realistic “worst‑case” hold conditions (time, temperature, container, mixing).
* Designing hold‑time studies that bracket expected process ranges and link results directly to CQAs and acceptance criteria.
* Defining and justifying both maximum and, where relevant, minimum hold times in process descriptions, validation, and control strategies.

For biologics and other sensitive products, this can be the difference between a process that is robust at commercial scale and one that repeatedly runs into unexpected variability and investigations.
