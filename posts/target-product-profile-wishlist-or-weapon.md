A Target Product Profile (TPP) is meant to describe the intended characteristics of a future product: indication, target population, route, dosing, efficacy, safety, and key pharmaceutical attributes. When used well, it acts as a strategic blueprint that links patient needs, regulatory expectations, and commercial realities into a single, evolving reference point for development decisions.

In practice, many TPPs behave more like wishlists. They capture everything teams would like the product to be, but are weakly connected to the evidence generation plan, CMC strategy, or real trade‑offs in development. As data emerge, the TPP may sit unchanged in a slide deck while clinical design, CMC scope, and commercial assumptions drift in different directions. The result is misalignment, rework, and late discovery that the product cannot deliver the value or label that stakeholders expected.

A TPP becomes a weapon when it is:

* Explicit about **minimum** versus **aspirational** targets (clinical, regulatory, and commercial), and when failing to meet “essential” criteria has clear consequences for the program.
* Structured to mirror likely labeling and access needs, so that endpoints, comparators, and quality attributes are chosen to support future claims and reimbursement, not just to “get through Phase 2.”
* Actively used to drive CMC and evidence generation: understanding what shelf life, presentation, dosing flexibility, or manufacturing scale are required to make the intended label and positioning credible.

For emerging biopharma and platform developers, a robust TPP signals strategic maturity to regulators, partners, and investors and improves the probability that development spend actually translates into a viable, adoptable product.
