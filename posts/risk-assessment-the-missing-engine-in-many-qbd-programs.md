Quality by Design (QbD) is often described in terms of tools: Design of Experiments (DOE), design space, and control strategies. But the core idea in ICH Q8 and Q9 is not “run more experiments”; it is to **focus** experiments and controls where they actually reduce patient and product risk.

When teams skip structured risk assessment, QbD efforts can quickly degrade into broad, unfocused DOEs that generate large data sets but limited insight. Every parameter looks “important,” many attributes are labelled “critical,” and the resulting design space is more a map of what was tested than a reflection of where genuine risk lies. The result is higher cost, more complexity, and a control strategy that can be difficult to justify to regulators.

ICH Q8(R2) and Q9 position risk management as integral to QbD:

* Start from the target product profile and critical quality attributes (CQAs).
* Use structured risk tools (e.g. PHA, Ishikawa, FMEA, risk matrices) to identify which material attributes and process parameters are most likely to impact those CQAs.
* Use DOE and further studies to **confirm and quantify** those relationships, refine risk rankings, and define a science-based design space and control strategy.

In this model, risk assessment is not a formality done after experiments. It is the step that determines which factors deserve experimental investment, what ranges to explore, and how to interpret outcomes in terms of patient and product risk.
