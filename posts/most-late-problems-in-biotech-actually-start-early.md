Not in the lab. In the definition of the product.

*Teams start generating data before answering a few basic questions:*

* What exactly is the product?
* How will it be delivered?
* What will regulators actually evaluate?

If those answers change later, the **development** path starts to wobble. Formulation changes. Tox assumptions shift. CMC strategies need rewriting.

> Suddenly the program looks inconsistent, even if the science is strong.

This happens *often* with newer platforms like RNA therapies, microneedle patches, or nasal delivery systems.

The lesson is simple: the earlier the product is clearly defined, the fewer surprises show up later.

Most development delays aren't caused by bad experiments. They start with a product that was never fully defined.
