Every strong regulatory submission tells a clear story: what was known, what was uncertain, what was tested, and how the data support the chosen path forward. That story does not start at IND drafting. It starts in the lab notebook.

Day-to-day experimental records are the first place where hypotheses, design changes, failed runs, and unexpected observations are captured. When those records are incomplete, inconsistent, or reconstructed after the fact, teams find themselves trying to build a coherent regulatory narrative from fragments. This is exactly where credibility, data integrity, and timelines begin to erode.

Current GLP and GxP expectations emphasize contemporaneous, original, attributable, and accurate records across the data lifecycle (ALCOA/ALCOA+ principles). In practice, that means experiments are documented in real time, rationale and deviations are explained, and raw data remain traceable to the person, method, and conditions that generated them. Good electronic or paper lab notebooks are not “extra paperwork”; they are the foundation for defensible claims about safety, quality, and mechanism.

When regulatory teams later need to justify a control strategy, explain a failed batch, or support a change, they are really asking one question: can we show a reliable progression of work from first principles to final decision? Programs that treat the lab notebook as the first draft of the submission have a much easier time answering “yes.”
