CMC technology transfer is one of the highest‑risk operations in pharmaceutical and biotech development. Moving from development to GMP manufacturing, or between sites and CDMOs, is often where gaps in process understanding, documentation, and alignment become visible in the form of deviations, failed batches, delays, and contentious comparability discussions.

Analyses of tech transfer programs and industry case studies highlight recurring themes: incomplete or inaccurate process documentation, shallow understanding of critical process parameters and material attributes, differences in equipment and facility design, weak data systems, and poor cross‑functional communication. For advanced modalities and complex biologics, even small changes in materials or operating conditions can create outsized impacts on product quality and validation outcomes.

The consequence is that a high proportion of transfers experience significant problems - not always labelled as “tech transfer failure,” but evident as validation setbacks, supply delays, expanded analytical work, and regulatory challenges. In many cases, the root cause is not the transfer activity itself, but CMC weaknesses that were never fully addressed earlier in development.

A more resilient approach treats tech transfer as a **continuation** of development, not a one‑time handoff:

* Capturing process knowledge in structured, transferable formats rather than relying on tacit experience.
* Designing scale‑down and scale‑up models that genuinely reflect receiving‑site equipment and constraints.
* Establishing two‑way communication between sending and receiving units so that feedback from the plant can refine the process and control strategy.
* Integrating regulatory expectations and comparability planning into tech transfer, rather than treating them as separate exercises.
