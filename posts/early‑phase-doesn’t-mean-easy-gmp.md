Clinical trial material is often described as “early” or “exploratory,” but regulators are clear: investigational products must still be manufactured and handled under robust GMP principles. Agencies such as FDA, EMA, TGA and WHO expect that clinical supplies are consistently produced, controlled, documented, and traceable in a way that protects participants and supports credible trial outcomes.



There is room for phase‑appropriate application – Phase 1 material does not require the full commercial‑scale Part 211 machinery – but this is not the same as a “GMP lite” regime. Core elements remain non‑negotiable: defined responsibilities, qualified facilities and equipment, validated or at least scientifically justified processes, sound analytical methods, documented batch records, deviation and change control, and secure packaging, labelling, storage and distribution.



Common pain points in clinical supply include:



Underestimating the GMP expectations for early batches, leading to gaps in documentation, traceability, or change control that become visible when trials expand or filings are prepared.



Treating clinical packaging, labelling, and distribution as logistics rather than GMP‑relevant operations, despite clear guidance that these must be controlled, documented, and auditable.



Relying on “one‑time” or manual workarounds that are hard to reproduce as more sites, countries, and combinations are added.



A more resilient approach builds a lean but real GMP framework for clinical supply:



Risk‑based definitions of what must be controlled, qualified, or validated at each phase, aligned with agency expectations.



Clear, usable batch documentation, material traceability, and deviation/change control that can scale with the program.



Integration of packaging, labelling, and distribution into the quality system, not as afterthoughts.
