Stability data often lag behind the rest of development. Formulations are advanced, dose and route decisions are made, and clinical planning progresses – while real-time stability quietly trails in the background. When programs then move to larger trials, new regions, or longer shelf-life needs, the lack of early stability insight becomes a major source of delay and anxiety.

In early development, it can feel “efficient” to do the bare minimum: one or two conditions, a limited set of tests, and a plan to “generate more later.” But stability is not just a regulatory checkbox; it is how you learn what your product can tolerate in the real world – temperature excursions, shipping, storage at clinical sites, and patient handling.

Programs that underinvest in stability early often run into familiar problems later:

* Shelf-life assumptions that cannot be justified when moving into larger or slower-enrolling studies.
* Unexpected degradation pathways emerging under long-term or stress conditions that were never explored when formulation decisions were made.
* Tight, conservative storage and handling requirements that reduce flexibility for sites, depots, and patients – or require urgent rework of labels and logistics.

A more resilient approach brings stability into the centre of early development:

* Designing stability studies that are modest in size but rich in information – including relevant stress conditions and realistic shipping/handling simulations.
* Looking at stability not only as “pass/fail at timepoints” but as a window into degradation mechanisms that inform formulation, packaging, and control strategy.
* Planning stability to support upcoming decisions – shelf life for the next trial, new presentations, new geographies – rather than simply meeting minimum guideline expectations.
