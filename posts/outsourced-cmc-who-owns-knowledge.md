Outsourcing CMC activities to CDMOs and CROs is now the default path for many development programs. It brings access to specialist expertise, established platforms, and infrastructure you do not need to build yourself. But one thing you cannot outsource is ownership of product and process knowledge.

Regulators consistently expect that the sponsor understands their own product, process, and control strategy – even when most or all execution is delegated to third parties. The sponsor remains accountable for quality, for regulatory submissions, and for the ability to explain how the product is made, controlled, and changed over time.

When knowledge sits mainly in slide decks, batch records, and inboxes at vendors, sponsors run into predictable problems:

* Long delays and inconsistencies when preparing regulatory filings or responding to questions.
* Difficulty explaining the rationale behind historical decisions and changes.
* Limited leverage when transferring to a new CDMO, adding a second source, or scaling up.
* A sense that “the CDMO knows the process, we just receive the reports,” which is not sustainable as programs advance.

A healthier model treats outsourced partners as extensions of the development team – not as the sole custodians of knowledge. That means:

* Defining what critical knowledge the sponsor must hold and maintain (and in what format).
* Setting expectations in contracts and ways of working for data access, documentation quality, and timely knowledge transfer.
* Creating simple, living summaries of product, process, stability, and analytical understanding that are maintained on the sponsor side.
* Making joint decisions visible and traceable, so the program can explain *why* as well as *what* was done.
