It’s tempting to treat scale-up as something that “we’ll worry about when the product succeeds.” In practice, many of the hardest scale-up problems are baked in much earlier – during preclinical and early clinical development, when processes, formulations, and controls are first defined. By the time a program reaches Phase 3, you’re no longer designing scale-up; you’re dealing with the consequences of every shortcut and assumption made along the way. 

True scale-up challenges are rarely just about equipment size or capacity. They are about whether the process, analytical methods, and control strategy can deliver consistent product across different scales, sites, and campaigns. If early decisions were made without considering future commercial needs, scale-up can trigger rework in process design, characterization, validation, and supply chain – exactly when time and tolerance are lowest.

Common signs that scale-up thinking started too late include:

* Discovering that raw material specs, process ranges, or equipment choices used in early phases don’t translate to commercial-scale manufacturing.
* Needing substantial process changes before validation because critical parameters were never properly understood or controlled at smaller scales.
* Facing unplanned tech transfers, multiple site strategies, or capacity constraints that the initial development work never anticipated. 

A healthier approach treats “phase-appropriate” as compatible with “scale-aware”:

* Build enough process and product understanding in early phases to know what must stay constant and what can flex as you grow.
* Choose platforms, unit operations, and analytics that are realistic for future commercial scenarios, not just convenient for early work.
* Use each phase to deliberately test and refine how the process behaves at different scales and conditions, rather than deferring those questions.
