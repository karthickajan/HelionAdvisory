Early‑phase dose escalation is often treated as a purely clinical design exercise: cohorts, rules, and models to find a safe and active dose. In practice, many of the hardest constraints come from CMC. Solubility limits, stability at higher concentrations, delivery route feasibility, device choices, and manufacturability can all narrow the dose range long before a “recommended” dose is identified.

When these constraints are not explored early, programs discover late that the clinically desirable dose cannot be formulated or delivered with the existing presentation. That can trigger emergency reformulation, new devices, bridging work, or re‑running parts of the development plan. Each of these adds time, cost, and regulatory complexity at exactly the wrong moment.

A more robust approach treats dose escalation design and CMC strategy as tightly linked. Preclinical and early CMC work can:

* Map realistic upper and lower bounds for feasible doses and concentrations
* Explore route and device options (e.g. IV vs SC, volume limits, viscosity constraints)
* Assess how stability, impurities, and manufacturability behave across the potential dose range
